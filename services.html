How TRT Works

Step one in understanding treatment is the mechanism. TRT supplies testosterone — usually by injection, sometimes by cream — to re-establish a stable level rather than to push a maximum value. It is a managed therapy because the introduction of external testosterone alters endogenous production, aromatization to estradiol, and binding-protein concentrations. Consequently, a protocol is derived from baseline labs and then revised against follow-up testing rather than fixed at the outset. This sequence is the reason supervision, not the prescription alone, determines outcomes; an unmonitored fixed dose can correct a number while leaving related markers unmanaged.

Testosterone Delivery Methods

Method selection is a substantive step. Injectable testosterone, intramuscular or subcutaneous, is the most widely used and the most readily titrated, with the lowest cost. Transdermal creams and gels yield steadier daily concentrations but introduce a transfer risk to others through skin contact. Implanted pellets provide several months of delivery but resist adjustment once placed. The handbook's guidance is that the method should follow the patient's labs, routine, and preference, and be integrated with any indicated adjuncts — anastrozole for estradiol or HCG for fertility — rather than applied uniformly.

Labs, Dosing & Monitoring

The diagnostic and dosing core is the laboratory panel. A complete evaluation reaches beyond total testosterone to free testosterone and SHBG, which together establish bioavailable hormone; estradiol, to gauge aromatization; DHEA-S; a full thyroid panel; fasting insulin and ferritin, to surface metabolic and iron contributors; and a CBC and PSA for safety. These results identify the type and cause of deficiency and fix the baseline for titration. Dosing opens conservatively and is revised from repeat labs and symptom review, generally quarterly, with hematocrit, estradiol, and prostate markers followed as standard monitoring.

Anastrozole, HCG & Fertility

Treatment is often a coordinated set of medications rather than testosterone alone. Where therapy elevates estradiol past its target, a small anastrozole dose can rebalance it; where estradiol falls too low, the testosterone dose is reduced instead. Because exogenous testosterone suppresses the pituitary signal to the testes and can impair fertility, men intent on preserving fertility may be given HCG alongside testosterone or an enclomiphene protocol as a substitute. These are prescription adjuncts applied selectively from the labs and the patient's goals. Their inclusion in up-front screening distinguishes a structured program from a dispensary.
